# Fair-use extract: Kirby Problem List: cited problems
**Source.** R. Kirby (ed.), Problems in Low-Dimensional Topology, in Geometric Topology (Athens, GA 1993), AMS/IP Stud. Adv. Math. 2.2, AMS/International Press, 1997.
**Supports.** GeometricTopology roadmap (the [Kir97, …] problem citations across layers).
*(Short attributed quotations of the problem statements, surrounding discussion summarised, for scholarly reference.)*

Each problem statement below is transcribed verbatim from the printed page indicated (with the original Unicode mathematical notation). Where a problem has lettered subparts, the whole problem statement is quoted but the lengthy "Remarks"/"Update" exposition is summarised, not quoted. Printed page numbers were verified against the rendered PDF (PDF page = printed page + 35).

## Problem 1.15 (p. 16)

> **Problem 1.15** Does every nontrivial knot K have Property P ; that is, does Dehn surgery on K always give a nonsimply connected manifold?

Asks whether every nontrivial knot has Property P (no Dehn surgery yields a simply connected manifold). The statement begins at the foot of printed page 16 and the accompanying Remarks list continues on page 17.

## Problem 1.19 (p. 20)

> **Problem 1.19 (Akbulut & Kirby)** **Conjecture**: If 0-framed surgeries on two knots give the same 3-manifold, then the knots are concordant.

A concordance conjecture: equal 0-surgeries should force the knots to be concordant.

## Problem 1.31 (p. 23)

> **Problem 1.31 (Y. Matsumoto)** Let 𝓗_A = {all knots in all homology 3-spheres which bound PL acyclic 4-manifolds, modulo homology bordism of pairs}.
>    Is the natural map C^PL → 𝓗_A an isomorphism?

Asks whether the natural map from the PL knot concordance group to the homology-bordism set 𝓗_A is an isomorphism.

## Problem 1.36 (p. 24)

> **Problem 1.36 (L. Taylor)** If a knot has Alexander polynomial equal to one, is it a slice knot?

Asks whether Alexander polynomial one implies sliceness. (The Remarks introduce "good boundary links" and ask, on printed page 25, whether a good boundary link is slice.)

## Problem 1.41 (pp. 28–29)

> **Problem 1.41 (A) (Akbulut & Kirby)** Let M_K⁴ be constructed by adding a 2-handle to B⁴ along a knot K with the 0 framing. Define the 0-shake genus of K to be the minimal genus of a smooth, imbedded surface in M_K representing the generator of H₂(M_K).
>      Does the 0-shake genus equal the 4-ball genus of K? Probably not.
>
> **(B) (Giffen)** What is the minimal genus of a smooth, imbedded surface in ∂M_K representing the generator of H₂(∂M_K)?
>      **Conjecture**: It equals the genus of K.

Part (A) compares the 0-shake genus of a knot with its 4-ball genus; part (B) asks for the minimal genus of a generating surface in ∂M_K, conjectured to equal the genus of K. (Statement begins on printed page 28 and continues onto page 29.)

## Problem 1.53 (p. 35)

> **Problem 1.53** Does mutation preserve the concordance type of a knot in S³ ? (Mutation is the operation of a knot which removes a tangle, twists it 180°, and glues it back in).

Asks whether mutation preserves knot concordance type.

## Problem 1.81 (pp. 59–60)

> **Problem 1.81 (Bleiler)** Let M be an oriented 3-manifold with ∂M = T², and let r be a slope (an isotopy class of unoriented, simple, closed curves) in T². Let M(r) = M ∪ B² × S¹ with ∂B² glued to the slope r (this is called an r-Dehn filling). One also refers to M(r₁) or M(r₂) as surgeries on N = M(r₁). Call two surgeries purely cosmetic if there is an orientation preserving diffeomorphism between M(r₁) and M(r₂), and chirally cosmetic if the homeomorphism is orientation reversing. Call two slopes, as well as the corresponding fillings, equivalent if there exists a homeomorphism of M taking one slope to the other.
>
> **(A) Cosmetic surgery conjecture:** Two surgeries on inequivalent slopes are never purely cosmetic. Equivalently, if M(r₁) = M(r₂) for inequivalent slopes, then the homeomorphism is orientation reversing.
>
> **(B) Conjecture:** There are no cosmetic surgeries, pure or chiral, on hyperbolic manifolds which yield hyperbolic manifolds.
>
> **(C) Conjecture:** Closed geodesics in a hyperbolic 3-manifold are determined by their complements (even allowing orientation reversing diffeomorphisms).
>
> **(D) Oriented knot complement conjecture:** If K₁ and K₂ are knots in a closed, oriented 3-manifold M whose complements are homeomorphic via an orientation-preserving homeomorphism, then there exists an orientation-preserving homeomorphism of M taking K₁ to K₂.

The cosmetic-surgery family of conjectures: part (A) (statement on printed page 59) is the cosmetic surgery conjecture; parts (B), (C), (D) on printed page 60 give the hyperbolic, closed-geodesic, and oriented knot complement versions.

## Problem 1.82 (p. 60)

> **Problem 1.82** Characterize those framed links in S³ which produce a connected sum of S¹ × S² 's.
>    **Conjecture:** Any link which can be obtained from the 0-framed unlink by handle slides (no stabilization by ±1 unknots necessary).

Asks to characterise framed links giving #(S¹ × S²); a multi-component generalisation of Property R.

## Problem 3.45 (p. 131)

> **Problem 3.45 Geometrization Conjecture (Thurston)**: An orientable, compact, irreducible 3-manifold M with incompressible boundary can be decomposed canonically along incompressible tori into geometric pieces.

States Thurston's Geometrization Conjecture. (The Remarks, pp. 131–132, list the eight model geometries and break the conjecture into parts (I), (II), (III) according to the structure of π₁.)

## Problem 3.51 (p. 142)

> **Problem 3.51 (Thurston)** Does a hyperbolic 3-manifold M have a finite cover which is a surface bundle over S¹?

The virtual fibering question for hyperbolic 3-manifolds.

## Problem 3.60 (pp. 146–148)

> **Problem 3.60 (C. C. Adams)** Hyperbolic 3-manifolds come in many flavors: orientable or not, closed or with totally geodesic boundaries (which are hyperbolic surfaces) or with one or more cusps (homeomorphic to S¹ × S¹ × ℝ or to K × ℝ in the non-orientable case where K is the Klein bottle); one can also consider orbifolds (see definition in Problem 3.46).
>
> **(A)** Determine the closed, orientable, hyperbolic 3-manifold of least volume.
>
> **(B)** Determine the non-orientable, closed, hyperbolic 3-manifold of least volume.
>
> **(C)** Determine the cusped, orientable, hyperbolic 3-manifold of least volume. A special case is to determine the hyperbolic knot in S³ of least volume.
>
> **(D)** Determine the n-cusped hyperbolic 3-manifold of least volume for n ≥ 3.

A family of minimal-volume problems for hyperbolic 3-manifolds across the orientable/non-orientable, closed/cusped cases. (Intro paragraph on printed page 146; the four parts span pp. 147–148 with intervening Remarks and figures.)

## Problem 4.82 (p. 225)

> **Problem 4.82 (Teichner)** Does (∗ℝℙ⁴)#(∗ℂℙ²) have a smooth structure?

Asks whether the fake non-orientable 4-manifold (∗ℝℙ⁴)#(∗ℂℙ²), which has zero Kirby–Siebenmann invariant, admits a smooth structure.

## Problem 5.2 (pp. 260–261)

> **Problem 5.2 (Lickorish)** Let K be a contractible finite 2-complex.
>
> **(A) Zeeman Conjecture**: K × I collapses to a point [1138,Zeeman,1964,Topology].
>
> **(B) Conjecture**: K 3-deforms to a point, i.e., there exists a 3-complex L such that K ↗ L ↘ pt.
>
> **(C) Conjecture**: The unique 5-dim regular neighborhood of K² in ℝ⁵ is B⁵.
>
> **(D₀) Andrews–Curtis Conjecture**: Any presentation of the trivial group can be changed to the trivial presentation by Andrews–Curtis moves [37,Andrews & Curtis,1965,Proc. Amer. Math. Soc.].

The Zeeman / Andrews–Curtis cluster of conjectures for a contractible finite 2-complex. Parts (A) and (B) are on printed page 260; parts (C) and (D₀) are on printed page 261. The Remarks note (A) ⇒ (B) ⇒ (C), (B) ⇔ (D₀), and that (A) implies the Poincaré conjecture.
