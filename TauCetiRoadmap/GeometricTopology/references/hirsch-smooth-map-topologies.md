## Summary
Chapter 2 §1 defines two topologies on Cʳ(M,N), the set of Cʳ maps M → N (initially with r finite). The **weak** (also called compact-open Cʳ) topology is generated by weak subbasic neighborhoods 𝒩ʳ(f; (φ,U),(ψ,V),K,ε), where a map g is close to f if, over a compact set K, the local representations of f and g together with their first k derivatives (k = 0,…,r) are within ε at each point. The resulting space is written C_W^r(M,N). The weak topology controls behavior over compact sets; when M is compact it coincides with the strong topology. The **strong** (also called fine or Whitney) topology uses a locally finite family of charts and per-chart positive numbers εᵢ, giving strong basic neighborhoods 𝒩ʳ(f; Φ,Ψ,K,ε); the resulting space is C_S^r(M,N). For non-compact M with N of positive dimension the strong topology is extremely large (not metrizable, no countable base at any point), but Baire's category theorem still holds in it. The C∞ topologies are defined as unions over finite r of the induced topologies.

## Key statements (quoted)
Definition of the weak topology (printed pp. 34–35):

> "If M and N are Cʳ manifolds, Cʳ(M,N) denotes the set of Cʳ maps from M to N. At first we assume r is finite.
> The weak or "compact-open Cʳ" topology on Cʳ(M,N) is generated by the sets defined as follows. Let f ∈ Cʳ(M,N). Let (φ,U), (ψ,V) be charts on M, N; let K ⊂ U be a compact set such that f(K) ⊂ V; let 0 < ε ≤ ∞. Define a weak subbasic neighborhood
>
> (1)     𝒩ʳ(f; (φ,U),(ψ,V),K,ε)
>
> to be the set of Cʳ maps g:M → N such that g(K) ⊂ V and
>
>     ‖Dᵏ(ψfφ⁻¹)(x) − Dᵏ(ψgφ⁻¹)(x)‖ < ε
>
> for all x ∈ φ(K), k = 0,…,r. This means that the local representations of f and g, together with their first k derivatives, are within ε at each point of K.
> The weak topology on Cʳ(M,N) is generated by these sets (1); it defines the topological space C_W^r(M,N). A neighborhood of f is thus any set containing the intersection of a finite number of sets of type (1)." — Hirsch, Chapter 2 §1, pp. 34–35.

Definition of the strong topology (printed p. 35):

> "If M is not compact the weak topology does not control the behavior of a map "at infinity" very well. For this purpose the strong topology is useful. (This topology is also called the fine or Whitney topology.) A base consists of sets of the following type. Let Φ = {φᵢ, Uᵢ}_{i∈Λ} be a locally finite set of charts on M; this means that every point of M has a neighborhood which meets Uᵢ for only a finite number of i. Let K = {Kᵢ}_{i∈Λ} be a family of compact subsets of M, Kᵢ ⊂ Uᵢ. Let Ψ = {ψᵢ,Vᵢ}_{i∈Λ} be a family of charts on N, and ε = {εᵢ}_{i∈Λ} a family of positive numbers. If f ∈ Cʳ(M,N) takes each Kᵢ into Vᵢ, define a strong basic neighborhood
>
> (2)     𝒩ʳ(f; Φ,Ψ,K,ε)
>
> to be the set of Cʳ maps g:M → N such that for all i ∈ Λ, g(Kᵢ) ⊂ Vᵢ and
>
>     ‖Dᵏ(ψᵢfφᵢ⁻¹)(x) − Dᵏ(ψᵢgφᵢ⁻¹)(x)‖ < εᵢ
>
> for all x ∈ φᵢ(Kᵢ), k = 0,…,r. The strong topology has all possible sets of this form for a base." — Hirsch, Chapter 2 §1, p. 35.

Relation between the two, and the C∞ case (printed p. 36):

> "The topological space C_S^r(M,N) resulting from the strong topology is the same as C_W^r(M,N) if M is compact. If M is not compact, however, and N has positive dimension, it is an extremely large topology: it is not metrizable and in fact does not have a countable base at any point" — Hirsch, p. 35–36.

> "We now define the spaces C_W^∞(M,N) and C_S^∞(M,N). The weak topology on C^∞(M,N) is simply the union of the topologies induced by the inclusion maps C^∞(M,N) → C_W^r(M,N) for r finite, while the strong topology on C^∞(M,N) is the union of the topologies induced by C^∞(M,N) → C_S^r(M,N).
> We give C^∞(M,N) the weak and strong topologies induced from C^∞(M,N)." — Hirsch, p. 36.
